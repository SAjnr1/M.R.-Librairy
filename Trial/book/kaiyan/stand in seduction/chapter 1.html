Chapter One


Adrian’s POV


I do not raise my voice. I don't need to. Authority is not something I perform, it is something I am.


Twelve men are sitting across from me, watching, waiting, and pretending like they were not already forming opinions about the future of my company. My grandfather’s company.


I built this. And yet—


“Mr. Vale,” Harrison says, adjusting his glasses like he was about to soften the blow. “We’ve reviewed your restructuring outline.”


I do not move. “And?”


Then, there is a pause. The type of pause that spells it out to me that, these men in suits have already decided on what to do with me behind my back. Traitors!


“It lacks… clarity.” He says, completing his sentence.


Of course it does. Clarity is what people ask for when they want control. When they don’t trust what they can’t predict.


My jaw tightens, but I keep my expression neutral.


Another voice joins in, cautious but bold enough to speak. “With your grandfather gone, the company is in a delicate position. We need reassurance that leadership remains—”


“Intact?” I finish for him.


They don't deny it or say that it is not what they are implying. There is just silence, confirming the finishing word I said to be true.


I push away from the table slowly, letting the weight of the moment settle exactly where I want it—on them. “You’re questioning my ability to run my own company.”


“It’s not personal,” someone mutteres and I almost laugh.


Everything in this room is personal. Before I can respond, the doors open. There is no knock or hesitation.


Every head turns to see this person who lacks mannerisms to ignore a knock on a door, including me.


And there she is. The expression on her face reads clearly that she is at the wrong place at the wrong time. Completely unaware of the storm she has just walked into.


She is slightly out of breath, with a folder clutched in her hand, her eyes scanning the room like she is trying to piece together where she went wrong.


“I think I’m in the wrong room,” she says.


I expected an apology or to see panic in her eyes, but there is none. She is just observing.


That's...interesting.


I mean, most people shrink under this kind of attention. Especially in this room, with me. But she does not.


My gaze sharpens slightly. “Clearly.”


A few of the board members shift, irritated by the interruption, but I barely register them anymore.


Because she is still standing there. Still looking.


Her eyes flick to the screen behind me—the presentation, the outline, the plan they just tore apart without understanding a single layer beneath it.


Her brows draw together. And then—
