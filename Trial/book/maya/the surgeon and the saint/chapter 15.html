Chapter Fifteen: Blood and Exit


The second bullet missed by inches. Euphoria felt the air snap past her cheek as Saint dragged her behind the kitchen island. She hit the floor hard, heartbeat roaring in her ears, mind blank except for one thought:


They’re trying to kill me.


Saint moved like instinct. Smooth, silent, lethal. He shoved a pistol into her hand.


“I don’t shoot people,” she hissed, breath tight.


“You do now,” he said. “Safety’s off. Point and pull if anyone who’s not me walks through that door.”


Her hands trembled. Saint peeked around the corner, read the shadows outside, then turned to her.


“There’s two. Maybe three. Pros. Suppressors. They’re not here to talk.”


“So what do we do?”


“We get out.”


Saint bolted from cover and fired twice through the broken window — controlled, deliberate. A grunt outside confirmed the hit.Then he grabbed Euphoria’s wrist and hauled her up. “Back door. Now!”


They sprinted down the hall, her bare feet slipping on hardwood as glass crunched behind them. The back exit opened into a narrow alley between buildings. Rain still puddled in the cracks of the pavement. Saint shoved her behind a dumpster, scanned the rooftops, and cursed under his breath.


“They’ve boxed us in.”


A dark figure dropped from the fire escape with the grace of a panther. Euphoria barely saw the gun before it was raised. Saint stepped in front of her.


Two shots rang out. The man hit the ground—dead before his body collapsed. Saint turned, grabbed Euphoria’s face in both hands. “You still with me?”


She nodded, but her eyes were wide. Shaking, breath ragged.


“I’m with you,” she whispered. “I just… I’ve never—”


“I know.” He touched her cheek. “But I have. Let me get us out.”


They ran again. Down the alley. Into the underground garage. Saint had a stash car — an old black Dodge Charger — hidden behind a row of dead vehicles under a tarp. He threw it off, popped the trunk, and handed her a jacket and new plates.


By the time they were on the highway, the sun was rising. Euphoria sat in the passenger seat, hands still wrapped around the gun, staring at her reflection in the window.


“You said you didn’t mean to fall in love with me,” she said suddenly.


Saint didn’t look at her. Just kept driving.


“That true?”


He was quiet for a long time. Then finally:


“It’s the truest thing I’ve ever said.”


She didn’t answer. But she didn’t let go of his hand when he reached for it, either.
