Chapter Twenty-Four: The Knife Between Us


Joaquin “Silk” Maddox didn’t storm in. He slithered in. With charm slick as oil and a grin carved from memory.


He didn’t come armed. He came invited. Because Euphoria agreed to meet him. At midnight. Alone.


Saint hated it — hated it so much it scared him — but she’d looked him in the eye and said, “Let me end what I started.”


So now, Saint sat in a backup sniper nest with a clean view of the abandoned hotel lobby where Silk waited. His finger on the trigger. His heartbeat in sync with hers.


And Euphoria? She walked in like a woman reborn — no longer mourning. But measuring. Silk stood when he saw her. Still tall. Still beautiful. Still poison.


“Grey eyes,” he said with that maddening smirk. “I dreamed of them in my coma.”


She didn’t blink. “I dreamed of your funeral. They let me choose the casket.”


He chuckled. “You always were sharp. But you didn’t kill me, baby. You just cut deep enough to matter.”


“You faked your death.”


“I needed a reset. You gave it to me. And now here we are. The surgeon and the ghost.”


She crossed her arms. “Why come back?”


He took a step forward. “Because the man you’re with now — he’s dangerous. Violent. And I know you, Euphoria. You don’t belong in his world.”


“I don’t?” she said softly.


Silk’s voice dropped, seductive. “You belonged in mine.”


That’s when her smile cracked through. Slow, dark, final.


“No, Joaquin. I survived yours. Now I belong in mine.”


She reached into her coat pocket and pulled out the USB Saint had given her — the one she’d quietly duplicated. It contained everything on Silk. Old deals. Blackmail. Proof of his betrayal to three different crime families. She tossed it onto the table between them.


“This is your coffin, Joaquin. I can hand it to the feds. Or you can take the third option.”


He stared at it, something truly nervous flickering behind his eyes for the first time, “What third option?”


Crack. The sound wasn’t a gunshot. It was the click of a pressure release — from under the table, where Euphoria had wired a hidden syringe. Silk looked down. To the dart in his thigh. Too late.


“What did you—?”


“Your blood’s always had thin walls. I just tore the last one open.”


He stumbled, eyes wide, knees buckling, “You… really did kill me.”


Euphoria knelt beside him as he gasped, hands shaking. “No,” she whispered. “This time, I let you watch it happen.”


By the time Saint reached her, Silk was gone — and peace had finally stopped running.


Saint didn’t ask if she was okay. He just held her. And she finally let herself break — not from grief. But relief. Because the ghost was gone. And the future was theirs.
