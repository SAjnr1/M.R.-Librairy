Chapter Six: The Fever


It started with a chill.


Saint had been quiet all evening. Too quiet. He hadn’t touched his food. Just sat at the window, his eyes distant and unreadable. Euphoria thought it was one of his moods again—brooding, withdrawn, staring like he could see ghosts between the rooftops.


But by midnight, he was on the floor.


She heard the thud—dull, heavy, wrong—and came running. He was crumpled in the hallway, forehead beaded with sweat, lips dry and cracked.


“Saint,” she whispered, kneeling beside him. “Hey. Look at me.”


His eyes fluttered open, but the focus wasn’t there. Just heat. Confusion. Pain.


“Shit,” she muttered, pressing a palm to his forehead. Burning. His body was shaking, the wound bright red around the edges. Infected.


He tried to speak but only managed a croak of breath. His hand reached for her like instinct. She took it.


“Don’t talk. Just breathe.”


She got him back to the couch with sheer will and leverage, whispering curses the whole way. Laid him flat. Stripped off his sweat-soaked shirt. The wound had gone angry—swollen, oozing. She grabbed her kit.


Antibiotics. IV drip. Cold compresses. Fever reducer. It was like being back in the ER, only this time the patient was a man who’d killed for a living, and for some damn reason, she was terrified of losing him.


Hours passed. She sat beside him, wiping his forehead again and again, whispering things she didn’t understand.


“You’re going to be fine. Don’t you dare die here. I’ll kill you myself if you die here.”


Sometime before dawn, he began to mutter. Words lost to fever. Fragments of another life.


“No... don’t make me... not the kid—”


She froze.


Then he whispered again. This time clearer.


“Euphoria…”


Her heart stuttered. He never said her name. Not since the first night. Always Doc, or nothing at all.


But now he said it like it meant something.


“Euphoria… stay…”


She sat there, holding his hand like it was the only thing tethering her to the room. His skin was burning hot, but his fingers clung to hers like they knew her already. Trusted her.


She didn’t move. Didn’t speak.


And didn’t let go.
