Chapter Four: The Way She Touched Him


The apartment was quiet except for the hum of the heater and the soft rustle of gauze.


He sat on her couch, shirtless, stitches stark against his side. The wound was healing, but slowly—healed more by her touch than the thread. Every time she leaned in to clean or re-dress the bandage, he had to remind himself to breathe.


Euphoria moved with the kind of calm that came from command. Her long white dreadlocks were pulled into a loose knot tonight, a few strands tumbling around her high cheekbones. She wore a fitted tank top and soft black lounge pants, and somehow, even in this casual stillness, she looked like the kind of danger men dreamed of but didn’t survive.


“You’re healing faster than I expected,” she said, taping the last piece of gauze.


“Must be your hands.” His voice was low, rougher than usual.


She raised an eyebrow. “That your way of flirting?”


He gave a crooked smirk, but it didn’t reach his eyes. “Would it work if it was?”


Euphoria stepped back, wiping her hands on a towel. “You’re not the first wounded man I’ve patched up.”


“But I might be the first you let stay in your home.”


That stopped her.


She didn’t answer, just crossed the room and poured herself a glass of water, as if avoiding the weight of his words. As if she hadn’t thought about it herself.


He watched her hips sway with every step, the way her silhouette burned itself into his memory. But it wasn’t just lust pulling at his chest—it was the way she never asked who he was. The way she treated him like a man instead of a monster.


“You saved my life,” he said finally. “And not just with a needle and thread.”


Euphoria glanced at him, eyes unreadable. “You’re still a mystery, you know. You haven’t told me your name.”


He hesitated, then said, “Call me Saint.”


She tilted her head. “Saint? You really expect me to believe that?”


“It’s what I’m called in my world.” He paused. “You’ve seen what I am. You just haven’t asked yet.”


Her voice was barely above a whisper. “Maybe I don’t want to know.”


They sat in silence. Something electric passed between them—an ache, a pull.


And then he stood, walked to her slowly, like he wasn’t sure what he was doing until he was standing close enough to feel her breath.


“You scare me,” he admitted.


She blinked. “Me?”


“Yeah,” he said, his voice rough. “Because you’re not afraid of me. And that means I might be real in your eyes... not just the killer I’ve always been.”


She stared at him a long moment, then whispered, “You’re more than you think.”


That night, he didn’t kiss her. He wanted to—but something told him it wasn’t time. Instead, he let the feeling bloom in his chest like something dangerous and new.


He was falling. And this time, it might not kill him.
