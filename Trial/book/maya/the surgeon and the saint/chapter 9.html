Chapter Nine: The Almost


Saint had been walking again.


At first just down the hallway, steadying himself with the wall. Then longer strides. Then barefoot in the kitchen, grabbing cereal and drinking juice straight from the carton like he owned the place.


“You’re really embracing the squatter aesthetic,” Euphoria said one evening, leaning in the doorway.


He was wearing one of her oversized hoodies. It hung off him like a borrowed life. “I like it here,” he said, with that half-smile she hadn’t figured out yet. “Smells like soap and clean sheets. You.”


Her heart skipped, but her face didn’t show it. “You’re getting sentimental. Maybe I should check your temperature again.”


But he wasn’t smiling now. He stepped a little closer. “You ever think about what happens when I’m healed?”


“Every day.”


“And?”


“I haven’t decided if I’m kicking you out or calling the cops.”


“Fair.”


There was a long, taut silence. They were standing too close. She could smell the sleep on his skin, the hint of antiseptic soap and something darker beneath. He looked at her mouth. She saw it.


“I’m gonna crash early,” she said, moving past him. But he grabbed her wrist—gently, two fingers on her pulse.


“You don’t have to be scared of me.”


She turned to face him. “I’m not scared of you. I’m scared of whatever happens if I stop being careful.” He let her go. And she walked away, but the truth was, she wanted to stay and he wanted to ask her to. Neither of them did. That night, she didn’t sleep, and neither did he. In their separate rooms, under the same ceiling, hearts beating like time bombs, they both lay awake and listened to the silence between them stretch and ache.
