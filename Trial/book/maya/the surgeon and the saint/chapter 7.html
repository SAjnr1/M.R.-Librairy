Chapter Seven: Pieces of the Past


By morning, the fever had broken.


Saint was drenched in sweat, breathing steadily, eyes closed but peaceful. Euphoria sat in the armchair beside the couch, arms wrapped around her knees, watching him like he might vanish if she looked away.


She hadn’t slept.


She told herself it was because she was monitoring his vitals. But really, it was something else—something she didn’t want to name yet.


When he finally opened his eyes, they found her instantly. He looked rough—skin pale, hair damp—but the sharpness was back. That ever-present alertness beneath the haze.


“You stayed,” he rasped.


Euphoria didn’t answer right away. She handed him a glass of water instead. “You were burning up. I don’t leave my patients halfway through.”


He took a slow sip, gaze never leaving hers. “That what I am? A patient?”


She stood. “What else would you be?”


Saint didn’t push it. Just nodded once, like that answer had both satisfied him and torn something open inside him.


Later, when he could sit up again, she brought him soup. He raised an eyebrow.


“I kill people for a living,” he said dryly. “You’re feeding me chicken soup like I’m your sick boyfriend.”


She almost choked on her water. “You wish.”


He smirked, but it didn’t last. Something heavy settled between them. Quiet, but not uncomfortable.


“Do you want to know what I used to do?” he asked suddenly.


Euphoria met his gaze, calm as stone. “Only if you want me to.”


“I don’t,” he admitted. “But I don’t want to lie to you either.”


She waited.


“I was a cleaner,” he said finally. “For the Syndicate. Which means I didn’t just kill. I erased. I made bodies vanish. People too. I’ve done things I can’t undo.”


Euphoria didn’t flinch.


“And you?” he asked after a long silence. “Why a trauma surgeon?”


She paused. “My mom died on a sidewalk. Stab wound to the abdomen. She bled out before the ambulance came. I was eight. I watched her die.”


He looked at her like he hadn’t expected real answers.


“I learned everything I could about trauma after that,” she continued. “I wanted to be the person who showed up in time.”


Saint leaned back, closed his eyes. “So we’re both ghosts, in our own way.”


She didn’t correct him.


Just sat there in the quiet with him, two people with hands soaked in blood—hers from saving lives, his from ending them.


And yet somehow, in the stillness between their scars, something started to grow.


Not love. Not yet.


But the ache that comes before it.
