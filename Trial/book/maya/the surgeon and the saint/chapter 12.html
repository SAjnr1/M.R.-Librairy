Chapter Twelve: The First Kiss


The storm outside had come suddenly. The rain poured against the windows, lightning cracking across the city skyline. The power flickered once, then cut completely, plunging the apartment into shadow. Euphoria lit two candles and placed them on the coffee table. Their soft glow danced on the walls, flickering between them like a heartbeat.


Saint was sitting there, legs stretched out, hoodie sleeves pushed to his elbows. He looked calm—but too still, like a man who had been bracing for something all his life. “I’ve been waiting,” he said softly.


“For what?” she asked.


“For this silence. This kind of quiet. The kind where no one’s bleeding and I’m not cleaning up a mess.”


She sat beside him on the couch. Their knees touched, barely. The candles made her skin glow. Her white dreadlocks were loose down her back, and the pale grey of her eyes looked nearly silver in the low light.


Saint turned to her, and for once, he didn’t say anything clever. Just studied her like she was a language he couldn’t quite read but had memorized anyway. “You’re not what I expected,” he said.


“Neither are you.”


He reached up, slow, and tucked a lock of her hair behind her ear. His hand lingered at her jaw, his thumb brushing the edge of her cheek. She didn’t move, didn’t speak, just let it happen.


When he leaned in, it wasn’t urgent. There was no hunger, no force, just a question in the space between them. And when their lips met—soft, tentative, warm—it felt less like a kiss and more like recognition. Like they’d known this would happen from the first moment. He pulled back first.


“Okay?” he asked, voice barely a breath.


Euphoria nodded, but her hands were shaking. Then she leaned in again and kissed him back. This time longer. Slower


The second kiss wasn’t cautious. It was quiet fire. The kind that built from everything unspoken, everything denied. Her fingers curled into the back of his neck, his hand sliding to her waist. Their bodies barely touched, but it didn’t matter. They felt everything, and when they finally pulled apart, they didn’t say a word, because nothing needed saying. The shift had happened, and there was no turning back now.
