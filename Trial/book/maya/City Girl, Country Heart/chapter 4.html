Chapter Four: The River Between Them


A week had passed. Seven whole days since the spoiled city girl with big blue eyes and a volcanic temper landed in the middle of nowhere, armed with sarcasm and designer sneakers.


Seven days since her high-pitched scream rattled the chickens. Since she’d nearly burned biscuits trying to “improvise” with almond milk. Since River had woken up to her blaring Shakira at 6 a.m. because “farmers need rhythm.”


And now?


She was… laughing. Laughing in the stables.


River stood outside the half-open barn doors, arms crossed, one booted foot resting against the frame. He hadn’t meant to eavesdrop. But the moment he’d heard her voice—soft, sweet, playful—he’d paused. Inside, Alara stood beside one of the horses, her long black ponytail swaying as she ran gentle fingers down its shiny neck.


“Well, hello, handsome,” she said to the brown stallion with white socks and a velvet nose. “Aren’t you just a dreamy slice of countryside magic?”


The horse nuzzled her arm, and she let out a soft giggle—light, unguarded, so unlike the razor-sharp sass she wielded like a sword. River felt something in his chest shift. She’d snuck down early, before breakfast. No camera in sight. No audience. Just her, a horse, and a light in her eyes that hadn’t been there when she’d arrived. He watched her lean in, nose to the horse’s nose.


“You’re my favorite,” she whispered. “Don’t tell the chickens. They’re already plotting my death.”


The horse snorted. She laughed again. And River… smiled.


She turned around, suddenly aware of being watched. Her eyes met his—those sea-deep blue eyes that looked like they could hold a storm or a secret depending on her mood. “How long have you been standing there?” she asked, brushing hay from her shorts.


“Long enough to know Midnight likes you,” he said, walking in.


Alara blinked. “Midnight? That’s his name?”.


“Yeah.” He patted the horse’s flank. “He doesn’t let just anyone pet him. He’s temperamental.”


She grinned, “Same.”


River gave her a sideways glance. “You doin’ okay?”


She shrugged, eyes flicking back to the horse. “Honestly? Yeah. I thought I’d go crazy out here. I miss sushi. And traffic. And...my facialist.”


She turned to him. “But this?” she motioned around the stable. “It’s not awful. It’s kinda… peaceful. Even when the chickens try to assassinate me.”


“You’ve been pulling your weight. Helping Grace. Not complaining every five minutes.”


She gasped. “Are you saying I’m—what’s the word—improving?”


He chuckled. “Don’t get cocky.”


“Too late.”


She leaned against the stable wall, arms folded, head tilted toward him. “So tell me something, cowboy. Why do you hate me?”


“I don’t hate you.”


“You sure?” She stepped closer, teasing. “Because I’m sensing a deep resentment.”


“I didn’t want to babysit a bratty city girl for six months.”.


“And now?”


He met her eyes. “Now I’m realizing you’re not just a brat. You’re...a disaster. But a funny one.”


She giggled, then snorted.


“Oh my God,” she said, horrified. “I snorted. I’m turning into a farm animal.”


River raised an eyebrow. “You sure you’re not Rhonda reincarnated?”


“Watch it,” she warned playfully, “or I’ll dump organic chicken feed in your bed.”


They stood there, the air still, thick with something electric. Unspoken. River shifted closer, close enough that she could smell the hay on him, the faint musk of sweat and sun.


He hesitated.


She didn’t.


She reached up and brushed a strand of hair from his forehead.


“I like you better when you’re not grumpy,” she said softly.


“I like you better when you’re not yelling.”


“I yell artfully.”


He smiled—actually smiled—and for the first time, it reached his eyes.


And just like that, Alara’s heart did something it had never done before.


It flipped.
