Chapter Five: Thunder, Tantrums, and Truth Bombs


It was supposed to be a quiet afternoon.


Alara had just finished helping Grace pick fresh tomatoes from the garden—wearing an off-shoulder top, of course, because even dirty work deserved drama—when the sky darkened like a moody teenager slamming a door.


Within minutes, fat raindrops started pelting down, soaking the fields and the clothes on her back.


“Get to the barn!” Grace yelled, already sprinting to the porch.


River was working near the fence line when he spotted Alara running barefoot across the muddy field, screaming at the heavens like they owed her money.


“OH HELL NO—IS THIS RAIN PERSONAL?!”


By the time she reached the barn, she was drenched head to toe, mascara running, hair plastered to her face, crop top clinging to every dangerous curve she possessed.


River followed her in, soaked but not nearly as dramatic.


“Where were you?” she snapped, shaking her arms like a wet cat. “I almost died of wetness.”


“It’s rain, not acid.”


She pointed a threatening finger. “I am not built for moist weather. I am not a sponge!”


“You’re more like a lightning rod,” he muttered, grabbing a dry blanket from the corner and tossing it at her.


She wrapped it around herself like royalty, huffing as thunder cracked outside. The barn groaned under the wind, and the horses neighed nervously.


River double-checked the doors, secured them, then turned back to find Alara curled up on a hay bale like a pissed-off cat.


“So dramatic.”


“I almost drowned,” she snapped.


“You ran through a garden. Not the Mississippi.”


She glared. “Do I look like someone who survives nature?”


“No, you look like someone who sues it.”


He dropped down onto the opposite bale and peeled off his soaked flannel shirt, revealing a white tank top that clung to his broad chest like sin and temptation. Alara looked away immediately.


Thunder clapped again.


She flinched.


He noticed.


“Scared?”


“I don’t do thunder,” she muttered. “I don’t like sounds that surprise me. Men who raise their voices. Balloons popping. And the IRS.”


He blinked. “That’s...a list.”


“What’s your list?” she challenged, adjusting the blanket. “What makes River the Brooding Farmer sweat in his flannel?”


He raised a brow. “Don’t flatter yourself. I don’t sweat.”


“You were literally dripping when you walked in.”


“That’s rain.”


“It’s liquid betrayal, either way.”


He chuckled, running a hand through his damp hair. “Fine. You want honesty?”


She perked up. “Do I? Always.”


“I hate failure. I hate not knowing what I want. I hate the idea of being stuck in the same place forever.”


She blinked.


“That got deep fast,” she whispered.


“You asked.”


“Well now I feel bad for calling you a ‘walking haystack with trust issues’ last Tuesday.”


He smirked. “You did, didn’t you?”


She grinned. “I say things when I’m cornered.”


Thunder rumbled again, louder this time. Alara whimpered before she could stop herself and huddled deeper into the blanket.


“Damn,” River said, moving beside her. “You really hate storms.”


She shrugged. “I used to love them… until I was nine and my parents fought through one. Screaming. Throwing plates. Mom crying. Dad walking out.”


River looked at her, really looked, and something softened behind his usual sarcasm.


“That sucks.”


“Yeah,” she replied, quiet. “I haven’t really liked the sound of thunder since. It’s like the sky's yelling.”


Silence stretched between them. The kind that wasn’t uncomfortable. Just real.


Then—because her mouth never stayed shut when it should—she said, “I also don’t like pickles, kale, or men who say ‘moist’ unironically.”


River laughed. Like, really laughed. Deep, low, surprising even himself.


“You’re insane.”


“I’m iconic. There’s a difference.”


They sat like that for a while—blanket between them, storm raging, hearts a little quieter than usual.


“I thought I’d hate you,” River said eventually, voice low.


“You did hate me.”


“Only for the first… week.”


“And now?”


He looked at her.


“I still think you’re spoiled, loud, a walking-talking storm...”


She smiled. “And?”


“And I think you make this place… interesting.”


Alara blinked. No sass. No comeback. Just a soft, stunned kind of stillness.


“Thanks,” she said, barely a whisper. “I think you’re...a horse with a trust fund.”


He blinked. “A what?”


“You act grumpy and stomp around, but deep down you’re just... secretly soft and expensive.”


He shook his head, grinning. “You’re impossible.”


“And you’re wet,” she added with a smirk. “Not that I’m complaining.”


He groaned. “You ruin every moment.”


“I enhance every moment.”


The rain kept falling.


But inside the barn, something had shifted.


The storm outside wasn’t the only one finally breaking.
