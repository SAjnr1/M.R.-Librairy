Chapter Six:Lady of the Land and Lord of Confusion


It was a warm Saturday afternoon.


The kind of golden, breezy day where the sun flirted with the clouds and the scent of fresh hay and sweat mingled in the air like some weird countryside cologne.


Alara stood at the edge of the cornfield, boots scuffed, flannel shirt tied around her waist, a straw hat perched on her head at a dramatic angle. She was watering rows of beans like she was born to do it—with flair, hips swaying to the Beyoncé song playing through her earbuds.


She had dirt on her knees, sweat on her brow, and a smile on her lips.


And yet—somehow—she looked drop-dead gorgeous.


River watched her from across the fence with a low sigh that came from somewhere beneath his ribs. She’d been driving him crazy all week with her playful banter, innocent sass, and how easily she’d adapted to life out here without even noticing she was adapting.


She was the storm in his slow life. And he hated it. And loved it.


“Yo! River!” a familiar voice called out from the road.


Three trucks pulled into the driveway, and out tumbled the cavalry—River’s childhood friends.


Josh, the tall flirty one. Cal, the lean sarcastic one. And Mitch, the big sweet one with a secret obsession for romance novels.


“Speak of the devils,” River muttered.


They sauntered over like it was a beer commercial—dusty boots, too much swagger, and loud voices echoing across the farm. And then…


They saw her. Alara turned at the sound of footsteps, tugged one earbud out, and blinked. The wind caught her hat. She reached up and adjusted it. A beam of sunlight hit her face like divine timing. And the boys… stopped moving. Literally. Josh froze mid-step, jaw dropping. Cal’s sunglasses slipped down his nose. Mitch walked into the fence. Thunk.


River rolled his eyes. “Hey,” he called, clearly annoyed. “Pick your tongues up. You’re embarrassing yourselves.”


Josh elbowed him. “You didn’t tell us you were hiding a whole goddess in your garden.”


“She’s not mine,” River muttered.


Alara tilted her head. “Uh… hello?”


Josh was the first to recover, flashing a grin and stepping forward like he was auditioning for The Bachelor: Barn Edition.


“I’m Josh. You must be… breathtaking.”


Alara blinked. “I’m...just watering beans.”


“She’s watering beans,” Mitch whispered like it was the most profound sentence ever spoken.


Cal cleared his throat, casually flexing as he leaned on the fence. “So, uh… you like tractors?”


Alara squinted, “Is that a pickup line?”


He coughed, “Maybe.”


Mitch pulled out a flower from his back pocket—he’d always kept one around for “romantic emergencies”—and handed it to her. “It’s a wild daisy. For you.”


She looked down at the flower. Then up at the three men now forming a loose triangle around her like a confused mating ritual.


“…What is happening right now?”


River crossed his arms, jaw clenched.


“They’re trying to impress you,” he said flatly. “It’s not going well.”


Alara stepped back, holding the daisy like it might explode. “Should I be flattered or call animal control?”


Josh laughed, eyes twinkling. “You’re adorable. City girl?”


“Born and branded.”


Cal nodded solemnly. “I’d drink oat milk for you.”


Mitch: “I’d read a 600-page Russian novel out loud. With emotion.”


River: “I’d personally like to be unconscious now.”


Alara looked around at the three farm boys slowly disintegrating into smooth-talking mush, then turned her gaze to River, eyebrows raised high.


“Are they always like this?”


“No. You broke them.”


“Should I apologize?”


“No. Let them suffer.”


She smiled and stepped closer to him, brushing past the others like it was nothing.


“River,” she said, voice like teasing honey, “you gonna save me from the groupies or what?”


He stared at her, pulse ticking. “You can handle yourself.”


“I can, but it’s more fun when you do it.”


Josh coughed into his fist. “Is it getting hot out here?”


Cal muttered, “I think he’s in trouble.”


Mitch whispered, “Is this a slow-burn enemies-to-lovers arc?”


Alara turned and handed Mitch back his daisy. “I’m keeping this for later. Might press it in my journal.”


Mitch nodded reverently, like she’d knighted him.


Josh looked at River. “You sure she’s not yours?”


River didn’t answer.


But his clenched jaw, twitching fingers, and the deadly glare in his eyes said everything.
