Chapter Three: Of Chickens and City Girls


Alara’s alarm screamed at exactly 5:00 a.m.


She smacked it without opening her eyes, groaning like someone had ripped her from a luxury spa and tossed her into a war zone. Because, in her opinion, they had.


She sat up slowly, groggy and confused.


Then she remembered.


The countryside. The chickens. River.


Her eyes snapped open, wild and betrayed.


“Who wakes up at five in the morning on purpose?” she hissed, dragging herself out of bed. Her silk robe trailed behind her like a wounded soldier.


Alara blinked. Then gagged.


“This is… fashion homicide.”


She picked the least offensive option: a white fitted tee, borrowed denim shorts that hugged her thick thighs, and an old flannel shirt tied around her waist. She still slipped on her big hoop earrings—farm or not, style was survival—and tied her long black hair into a ponytail.


When she looked in the mirror, she winced. But also… she kinda looked like a hot country singer.


If that singer had never seen dirt in her life.


The morning was brisk and golden, dew still clinging to the grass. Birds chirped cheerfully.


Alara stumbled out onto the porch like a hungover raccoon. She blinked against the rising sun and found River already standing by the barn, arms crossed, work gloves on, and a shovel in one hand.


“Good morning, sunshine,” he drawled.


“Don’t talk to me before 10 a.m.,” she muttered.


“Don’t worry,” he said, tossing her a pair of gloves, “you won’t be conscious much longer.”


She caught the gloves. “What kind of serial killer smile is that?”


He grinned wider. “The kind that says, you’re about to meet the chickens.”


The coop was… hell.


Absolute hell.


There were feathers everywhere, the smell of warm poop, loud clucking, and one rooster that Alara swore was possessed by a demon.


River opened the coop door like a cowboy facing a saloon showdown.


“Go ahead, Princess.”


Alara hesitated, peering into the feathery chaos. “They’re… alive.”


“Smart observation.”


“And they see me.”


“They have eyes.”


She narrowed hers. “If one of them flies at me, I’m suing.”


“You’re on Grace’s land. You’ll be suing a biscuit.”


With a dramatic sigh and silent prayer, Alara stepped inside the coop.


The chickens scattered like gossip at brunch.


River handed her a small basket. “Collect the eggs. Easy. Don’t piss off the hen on the left—she’s feisty.”


“Which left? My left or your left?”


He walked off, already chuckling.


She glared after him. “I hope you trip and fall into a cow butt.”


The first egg was easy.


The second was guarded by a bird who pecked the back of her hand like it was trying to send a message.


“Excuse you!” Alara gasped. “I am a woman of dignity!”


The third… didn’t exist. Because by the time she reached for it, the hen flapped its wings so violently that Alara let out a scream and fell backward into a pile of hay, her long legs kicking up.


River, unfortunately, had chosen that exact moment to check in.


He leaned against the coop door, arms crossed, watching her flail like an upturned turtle.


“You havin’ fun?”


“I was assaulted,” she gasped. “That bird has anger issues!”


“That’s Rhonda,” he said. “She doesn’t like new people. Or people who wear perfume that smells like strawberries.”


“I smell like heaven,” Alara snapped, trying to stand but slipping again.


He walked over, reached down, and with a roll of his eyes, hauled her up by the waist like she weighed nothing.


Her breath caught. She hated that her breath caught. “Thanks,” she mumbled, brushing hay off her butt.


“Try not to die before breakfast,” he said, walking away again.


By the time she stumbled back into the kitchen, she was covered in feathers, hay, and chicken rage. Grace took one look at her and burst out laughing.


“Sweet Jesus, you look like a reality show gone wrong.”


“I was brave,” Alara said, dropping the egg basket on the table like she’d just come back from war. “One of them pecked me! And the rest just… clucked. Judged me.”


Grace handed her a biscuit. “Welcome to your first day on the farm.”


“Do I get a trophy?”


“Eat your carbs, darling.”


River entered next, sweaty, shirt damp, pushing his sleeves up to his elbows. Alara did not stare....much


“Chickens like you,” Grace said.


“Because they see me as one of their own,” Alara muttered.


River smirked.


“Go on, laugh it up, farm boy,” she snapped, mouth full of biscuit. “I’ll be queen of the coop soon.”


“You’ll be lucky if Rhonda doesn’t file a restraining order.”


Later that day, as Alara sat in the swing chair on the porch, sipping iced tea and massaging her sore thighs, she noticed something strange. She wasn’t miserable. She hadn’t checked her phone in hours. She hadn’t posted a selfie, or ordered anything online, or talked to anyone but chickens and two people who made her feel like more than a spoiled city girl. She looked out at the field, golden and endless. For the first time, she wondered if the country wasn’t the punishment she thought it was. Maybe… it was her beginning.
