Chapter Nine: Toes, Tumbles, and Trouble


It was late afternoon, just after chores but before dinner—the golden hour when the farmhouse glowed and the world felt slow and sleepy. Alara had just finished journaling under the willow tree, writing a scathing essay titled “10 Reasons Why Snakes Are Satan’s Shoelaces.” Her hair was a little windblown, her lips cherry-glossed, her skin glowing like caramel warmed by the sun. She was humming, barefoot (as usual now), and heading inside through the back porch doorway.


River had just come back from the barn, sweaty, shirt half unbuttoned, boots dusty, and his dark hair messy in the way that made her chest tighten more than she wanted to admit. They didn’t know they were about to collide. Literally.


---


Inside the kitchen door, she stepped forward. He stepped in.


“—Oh SH—!”


“Watch your—”


She stubbed her toe hard on the edge of the porch step and yelped.


“GOD BLESS AMERICA’S CORN!”


She stumbled forward with a comically loud gasp, lost all balance, and slammed into a wall of River’s chest with the velocity of a drunk cannonball. River caught her again—reflexes like sin. But her arms flailed up around his neck. And her face… Her face was right there. So close. Too close.


Their lips brushed. Soft, barely-there but definitely real. Time froze. Her breath caught. His arms stiffened around her waist. Neither of them moved. The kiss was short—but it felt like someone had dropped a lightning bolt down both their spines.


---


She jerked back with a gasp, eyes wide like she’d just accidentally kissed a volcano.


“WHAT—WHAT JUST—WHAT THE HELL—” she stammered.


River blinked, frozen, jaw clenched, clearly processing like a broken vending machine. “You… you tripped,” he said, voice a little rough.


“ON THE DOORFRAME, NOT YOUR LIPS.”


“Not my fault you landed there.”


She was fuming now, face flushed, toe still throbbing. “You—you caught me!”


“You always fall!”


“I DON’T ALWAYS FALL!”


“You’ve fallen into me four times this month.”


“Yeah, well—YOU’RE BUILT LIKE A DAMN TREE!”


“Maybe you should stop flinging yourself around like a tornado!”


They were both breathing hard, inches apart, voices too loud, eyes locked in pure chaos and confusion.


Then—


Silence.


Her chest rose and fell. He was still holding her. She realized it. He did, too. He slowly let go. She stepped back. Way back.


“I need… I need iced tea,” she said breathlessly. “And a foot massage. And a lobotomy.”


River swallowed. “I’m gonna go… exist elsewhere.”


“Please do.”


They turned in opposite directions and walked off—Alara toward the fridge like it owed her answers, and River out the door like he was escaping a war zone.


---


Outside, River leaned on the porch post, head tipped back, muttering to himself. Inside, Alara stood at the sink, one hand over her mouth. “Okay,” she whispered. “So we’re kissing people now? Apparently that’s a thing. Fine. Fine. It was an accident. Lips are squishy. Proximity. Physics. No feelings. No danger. I’m fine. Totally. Fine.”


She opened the fridge, stared blankly, closed it again.


Then whispered to herself: “I kissed the haystack. And I liked it.”
