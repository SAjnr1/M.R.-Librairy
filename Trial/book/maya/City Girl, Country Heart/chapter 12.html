Chapter Twelve: Sunsets and Sand in the Hourglass


The days started passing faster. The sun seemed to set a little sooner each evening. The air felt different now—not just warm, but thick with something unspoken. Every moment tasted a little sweeter, a little sadder. Alara knew why. Her summer was ending....Fast.


---


She sat on the porch one evening, wrapped in one of River’s flannels, sipping sweet tea as she watched him fix the fence near the cornfield. He looked good like that. Sunlight hitting his skin. Muscles flexing just enough to make her legs twitch.


And that peaceful focus in his eyes—like the world wasn’t rushing forward without permission. She didn’t want to break the moment. Didn’t want to remind him. But he already knew. But he already knew. Everyone did.


She’d gotten a call from her mom that morning. School was starting again in two weeks. City life waited. Her room. Her friends. Her father. And yet… Everything in her ached to stay. To wake up to the sound of roosters and regretful goats.


To hear Grace humming while biscuits baked. To feel River’s hand sneak onto hers under the dinner table like it was a secret they shared with the stars. She sighed, setting down her glass. And that’s when he turned. Their eyes met. He knew.


---


Later that night, they lay on the bed of his truck, parked at the edge of the wheatfield. No music. No mosquitoes. Just them. The stars above seemed quieter than usual. River lay on his back, hands behind his head.


Alara curled up beside him, cheek pressed to his chest. “Tell me something stupid,” she whispered.


“Like what?”


“Like how this ends.”


He didn’t answer right away.


Then:


“You go back.”


“I don’t want to.”


He turned his head, looked at her. “You sure?”


She blinked up at him. “I came here with twelve suitcases and an attitude. I’m leaving with dirt under my nails and your stupid goat following me everywhere.”


He chuckled softly. “Regret does love you.”


“Regret has taste.”


She fell silent for a long time.


Then added, voice cracking, “I didn’t expect this...you...any of it.”


River didn’t speak. He just ran his fingers through her hair, slow and careful, like touching something he wasn’t ready to let go of.


“You changed me,” she whispered. “You ruined the city for me.”


He breathed out. “You lit this place on fire.”


“I might cry when I leave.”


“I might let you.”


She looked at him, “I don’t want to be a summer memory.”


“You won’t be,” He kissed her forehead. Then her cheek. Then her lips. Soft. Like a promise.
