Chapter One: City Lights, Daddy’s Girl


Alara Nkemdi Lovelace had no idea her world was about to crumble.


Her long, black hair was swept up in a messy high ponytail, not a strand out of place, and her glossy blue eyes were hidden behind designer sunglasses the size of a small country.


A phone was glued to her ear.


“No, bestie—listen to me, listen,” she said, lips slick with cherry lip gloss as she twirled a lock of hair. “If he doesn’t text back within ten minutes after seeing your message, he doesn’t respect you. Delete. Block. Burn.”


On the other end, her best friend was sobbing over a boy again.


Alara rolled her eyes, took a bite of the croissant her dad’s personal chef had plated for her, and continued, “You are not just some girl, okay? You’re an experience. A luxury. He should be grateful you even looked his way, let alone sent him a meme.”


The sobs slowed.


“That’s right. Cry it out. Now go pour some wine and stream some true crime. You’ll feel better.”


Her mother’s voice sliced through the air like a blade dipped in honey. “Alara Nkemdi Lovelace.”


Alara flinched slightly, then groaned, “Ugh, I’ll call you back. My mother is summoning me like I’m Cinderella.”


She pulled her sunglasses down her nose and arched one perfectly waxed brow. “Yes, Queen?”


Her mother, draped in a crisp white kaftan and sipping green tea, stood with arms folded. Her elegance was effortless, but the scowl on her face was unmistakable. “We need to talk.”


“That sounds dangerous,” Alara said, sitting up. “Is this about the Marc Jacobs order? I swear I thought the coat was already on sale. Honest mistake.”


“This isn’t about your shopping addiction, although that’s a conversation we’ll definitely revisit,” her mother replied tightly. “It’s about your future. Your behavior. Your… spoiled princess syndrome.”


Alara blinked. “Excuse me?”


“You haven’t lifted a finger this summer.”


“I’ve lifted several fingers. To scroll. To FaceTime. I even lifted two yesterday when I flipped off that taxi driver who honked at me.”


Her mother looked like she might combust. “You are nineteen, Alara. Nineteen. A grown woman. And you’ve spent this break like a house cat in heat — sleeping, eating, complaining, and ordering things you don’t need.”


“That’s not fair,” Alara pouted. “I’m more of a panther.”


“I’m serious.”


“So am I.”


Her mother sat beside her and lowered her voice. “Your father and I had a conversation last night. It’s time we do something drastic.”


Alara narrowed her eyes. “Define ‘drastic.’ ”


Before her mother could reply, her father entered the room, tall and warm like golden summer light, his suit jacket flung over his shoulder and his tie loose.


“Hey, pumpkin,” he greeted her, kissing her cheek.


“Hi, Daddy,” she smiled, immediately melting into him. “Your wife is trying to ruin my life.”


He laughed. “Let me guess. The plan?”


“She said the word ‘drastic’ with her whole chest. I don’t like it.”


Her father sighed and sat across from her. “We think it would be good for you to spend some time away from the city. Learn a different pace. Get a little dirt on those fancy nails of yours.”


Alara stared, waiting for the punchline. “...This is a prank, right? Where are the cameras?”


“No prank,” her mother said. “You’re going to the countryside.”


There was a long, stunned silence.


Alara blinked.


The room shrank around her.


“I’m sorry, the what side?”


“The countryside. Fields. Goats. Fresh air.”


“Fresh air? Oh my God.” She pressed a hand to her heart like it might stop beating. “Why not just send me to prison?”


Her dad chuckled. “You’ll be staying with my old friend Grace. She has a farm. A lovely place.”


Alara laughed, hysterical now. “A farm? Me? I don’t even wash my own hair! I pay someone!”


“She has a son a year older than you,” her mother added. “He’ll show you around. Keep you out of trouble.”


“Oh, great. So you’re sending me to live with cows and a boy who probably smells like manure and Axe body spray.”


Her father’s tone softened. “You’ve been struggling, Alara. Restless. Unfulfilled. Maybe what you need isn’t more parties or clothes. Maybe you need to rediscover… simplicity.”


“Daddy,” she whimpered, big blue eyes wide and shiny. “Please don’t do this to me. I’ll get a job! I’ll volunteer! I’ll walk the neighbour’s cat!”


He smiled and pulled her into his arms. “It’s only six months. You’ll be back before the holidays. Think of it like… rehab for rich girls.”


“I hate this family.”


“You love this family.”


“Not anymore.”


“You’ll thank us later.”


“Doubt it.”


“Come on, Pumpkin. Give it a try.”


She sniffed. “Only because you called me pumpkin.”


Three Days Later


Alara stood at the edge of a gravel road surrounded by endless green fields, the city skyline long behind her, and the stench of cow manure filling her nostrils.


Her white luggage looked tragically out of place.


A red pickup truck rumbled up the path. The driver honked once and leaned out of the window. He was tall, broad, tanned from the sun, with a strong jaw, dark eyes, and a scowl that could kill a flower.


“You Alara?” he called.


She gave him a slow up-down and narrowed her eyes.


“And you must be the son I was warned about.”


He climbed out and popped the trunk. “Name’s River. Let’s get this over with.”


She gasped. “Excuse me, River? Like the body of water? Were your parents high?”


He rolled his eyes. “You got a problem with nature names, city girl?”


“I have a problem with everything right now.”


“Then you’re in the right place.”


She stood firm as he lifted her bag into the truck bed with one arm. Show-off.


“This doesn’t mean I’m happy to be here,” she said as she climbed in beside him.


“Oh trust me,” he muttered, “the feeling’s mutual.”


As they drove off, the sun dipped low behind the hills, and Alara Lovelace realized she was about to face the longest six months of her life.


What she didn’t know—what neither of them knew—was that buried beneath all that sass, sharp tongues, and snide remarks…


Was the start of something wild, real, and completely unexpected.
