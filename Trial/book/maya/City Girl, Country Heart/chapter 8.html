Chapter Eight: Snakes, Sass, and Second-hand Embarrassment


The world came back to her slowly.


Warmth.


Arms.


A hand brushing her hair. A familiar scent—grass, sweat, and something… River. Her lashes fluttered. She opened her eyes to find herself cradled in River’s lap, one of his hands on her waist, the other gently supporting her neck.


“You’re okay,” he said, voice low, eyes searching hers. “It’s gone.”


She blinked, trying to gather her thoughts—or, at the very least, some dignity. Then she remembered. The snake, the scream, the leap, the cling.


“Oh my God,” she groaned, slapping a hand over her face. “I did not just pass out in public like a 19th-century widow.”


“You did,” he said. “Right after accusing a baby snake of being the Devil.”


“I am Ophidiophobic,” she growled, sitting up abruptly and wobbling before catching herself. “That’s a real condition, thank you very much. I read about it. And I am deeply afflicted.”


River raised a brow. “You’re also dramatic as hell.”


She glared at him. “Don’t you dare tell Grace about this.”


---


Later That Night…


Grace had prepared a dinner worthy of the gods—roasted corn, fried green tomatoes, barbecued chicken thighs, cornbread, and a bowl of creamy potato salad that could probably solve world peace. Alara, however, was still sulking, poking at her food with unnecessary aggression while throwing side-eyes at River, who sat across from her looking entirely too pleased with himself. Josh, Cal, and Mitch were dying to bring up the earlier drama.


Grace raised a brow over her iced tea. “So… I hear someone had a little incident today?”


River opened his mouth, but—


“I’ll tell it,” Alara said quickly, straightening her spine like a dignified chaos queen about to give a TED Talk. “Because someone,” she cut a sharp glance at River, “doesn’t know how to narrate with nuance.”


River muttered, “Here we go.”


Alara slammed her fork down with flair. “There I was, in the wild, surrounded by thorns, alone, vulnerable, beautiful.”


“You were with four people and had lip gloss in your pocket,” River said flatly.


“Surrounded by danger!” she shouted, ignoring him. “The air grew cold. The birds flew away. The grass hissed. And then… from the underworld itself…”


Josh covered his face.


Mitch whispered, “This is already better than Netflix.”


“…a serpent! Long, evil, malevolent and possibly a reincarnated demon.”


“It was eight inches long and afraid of you,” River said dryly.


“I screamed with the raw honesty of a woman fighting for her life. I flew, River.... I ascended....like an angel.”


“You launched yourself at me like a toddler attacking a bounce house.”


She scowled. “Because you were the only one qualified to hold me! Everyone else looked like they’d faint before I did.”


Grace tried and failed to hide her laughter.


“So then what happened?” she asked.


“I… may have fainted briefly due to emotional stress,” Alara said with a wave. “But when I woke up, I was in River’s arms. Again. So, really, it’s his fault.”


“How is that my fault?”


“You give strong arms and savior energy. It’s misleading.”


River blinked. “...What?”


She took a sip of water, eyes narrowed. “I’m suing the ecosystem.”


Mitch raised his hand. “Petition to ban snakes from Hollowspring?”


“Seconded,” Cal said.


“Thirded,” Alara said solemnly, placing a biscuit on her heart.


River groaned. “I hate all of you.”


Alara smiled sweetly. “And I hate snakes. Yet here we are.”
