Chapter Thirteen: The Goodbye That Wasn't


The morning sun rose gently, too gently. Birds chirped like they didn’t know her world was cracking open. Alara stood by the front porch with her suitcase beside her, River’s flannel draped over her shoulders, and her hair tied in a lazy bun that still somehow looked like a music video. Grace hugged her tight, eyes glistening, flour on her apron like always.


“You changed my kitchen,” she said softly.


“You changed my heart,” Alara whispered back.


River stood a few feet away, arms crossed, jaw tight, pretending he wasn’t dying inside.


He hadn’t slept last night. Neither had she. Too much had been said in silence. Too much had been felt in kisses, in glances, in held hands beneath the stars. She’d told him she’d come say goodbye. She hadn’t said how. And then—


A car pulled into the drive. Her father stepped out—tall, clean-cut, warm smile on his face. “There’s my girl.”


Alara’s breath caught in her chest.


He waved to Grace, extended a hand to River, who shook it with forced politeness and said, “Sir.”


“Thank you for taking care of her,” her father said.


River nodded once. Alara stood there frozen, eyes darting between them.


Her dad opened the car door. “Come on, Pumpkin. We’ve got a plane to catch.”


She reached for her suitcase. She paused. Something in her body refused. Something in her heart screamed. And then—


She dropped the suitcase. And ran. “Alara?” her dad called. She didn’t look back.


She ran down the steps barefoot, bolting across the gravel, her chest heaving, her flannel flapping behind her like wings. River barely had time to react before—


She crashed into him. Full force. Arms around his neck. Legs around his waist. She kissed him hard. Right there in the middle of the gravel path, with the sun burning over the wheat and Grace softly gasping on the porch. River caught her like he was built for this—like this was the moment he’d been born for.


She pulled back, tears in her eyes. “I can’t just walk away.”


He stared at her, stunned, voice breaking. “You came back.”


“I never wanted to leave.”


“You ran,” he said, smiling just a little.


“Like a rom-com.”


“You jumped.”


“Like a maniac.”


He kissed her again, slower this time, both of them trembling, both of them breathless. Behind them, her dad stood at the car, smiling sadly, not moving. Grace wiped her eyes with the edge of her apron. Alara clung tighter. “I love you,” she whispered suddenly, voice barely there.


River froze.


Then whispered right back: “I’ve been trying not to.”


She smiled, pressed her forehead to his.


“Stop trying.”
