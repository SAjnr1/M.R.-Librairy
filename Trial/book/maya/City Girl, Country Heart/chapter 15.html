Chapter Fifteen: Dancing Between the Dishes


The apartment was quiet. The kind of quiet that only came after a long, loud day—after Zara left (reluctantly), after River took a long, hot shower, and after Alara finally, finally stopped pretending she wasn’t shaking from seeing him again.


She was in the kitchen, barefoot in shorts and a silk tank top, hair piled on top of her head in a messy bun. Her favorite playlist hummed low from her phone speaker, a warm mix of R&B, soft soul, and acoustic magic.


River stepped in, a little unsure, towel-drying his hair. She looked up from the stove. Their eyes locked.


Her lips curled into that smile—the one that never needed permission. “You’re staring,” she said softly.


“You’re glowing,” he replied, even softer.


She laughed once, breathy. “Must be the garlic butter.”


He stepped closer, “Dinner smells amazing.”


“You’re welcome. I used half a stick of Grace and two spoons of missing-you.”


He slid behind her without touching, just close enough that she could feel him.


She reached for a plate. He grabbed it first. Their fingers touched. She turned. Their faces were too close.


“Are we gonna pretend we’re not seconds away from doing something cinematic?” she whispered.


He blinked. “Cinematic?”


She raised a brow. “You’re wearing grey sweatpants in my kitchen. We have jazz playing. And wine breathing. The universe is begging us to dance.”


He tilted his head. “You don’t even like slow dancing.”


“I didn’t.”


She slid her hands up to his shoulders.


He rested his hands on her hips.


And then—


They moved. No choreography. No audience.


Just two people swaying slowly on a tile floor, wrapped in half-whispers and full heartbeats.


He pulled her closer. She melted into him. Her head rested against his chest, right over the steady thump of his heart. He breathed her in like she was air after a drought.


She tilted her chin up. “City life looks good on you.”


“I’m not here for the city,” he murmured, brushing his lips against her forehead. “I’m here for the girl who burned down my farm life in denim shorts.”


She smirked, “It was the crop tops, wasn’t it?”


“Definitely the attitude.”


“Mm. And the goat.”


“Regret did most of the work.”


They both laughed. Then the music shifted to something softer. Something sweeter.


And they stopped laughing. He cupped her jaw, his thumb brushing her cheek.


She looked up, lips parted. He kissed her.


Not urgently.


Not hungrily.


But like a man who finally had everything he never thought he needed.


Her fingers tangled in his shirt. His hand slid into her hair.


When they finally broke apart, she whispered, “Don’t leave.”


“I won’t,” he said. “Not unless you push me out the window.”


“No promises.”


“Worth the risk.”


They danced until the garlic burned.


And even then, neither of them cared.
