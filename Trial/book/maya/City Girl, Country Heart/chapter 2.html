Chapter Two: The Chicken Coop Princess


By the time the rusty red pickup truck rolled to a halt in front of a white farmhouse with blue shutters and a wraparound porch, Alara had already mentally written her will.


She stepped out, carefully, like the gravel might explode under her spotless sneakers. She glanced around—swaying wheat fields, wooden fences, a wind chime singing in the breeze—and sighed as dramatically as her lungs allowed.


It was quaint, simple and straight out of a Hallmark movie. The kind where the city girl either found herself… or died of mosquito bites.


River slammed the truck door. “You comin’, or you gonna stand there until the cows come home?”


She shot him a tight smile. “I’m trying to emotionally prepare. Some of us are sensitive.”


He muttered something about “divas and delusions” and walked ahead.


Alara trailed behind him, adjusting her crop top and flicking her ponytail like armor.


As they approached the porch, a woman stepped out onto the front steps. She wore jeans, boots, a loose linen blouse, and an apron stained with flour and something dark. Her grey-streaked hair was tied in a bun, and her smile—wide, motherly, kind—was the first warmth Alara had felt since leaving the city.


“There’s my girl,” the woman beamed.


Alara blinked, “You… must be Grace.”


“And you must be the firecracker I’ve been hearing about.” Grace opened her arms.


Without hesitation, Alara dropped her bags and fell into the hug. She hadn’t realized how much she missed the scent of home-cooked food and soft, familiar warmth.


Grace hugged her like she meant it. “Look at you. Lord have mercy—you’re gorgeous. Your mama sent me a picture, but it didn’t do you justice.”


Alara grinned, “You can keep talking.”


“Polite, too.”


Behind them, River scoffed, “You should’ve heard her threaten to set my truck on fire for being ‘aesthetically disrespectful.’ ”


“Because it was! Who paints a truck that shade of crusty ketchup?”, Alara fired back.


Grace laughed, “Oh, I see you two are getting along just fine.”


River raised a brow, “Define 'fine'.”


Grace waved him off. “Go bring in her things, boy. And don’t break her makeup bag again like you did mine last year.”


“Still holding that over my head?”


“Until I die. Now move.”


Alara smirked as River sulked toward the truck.


“Come in, sugar,” Grace said, leading her inside. “Let me show you your room and fix you something to drink. I made lemonade the old-fashioned way. With real lemons.”


Alara’s eyes lit up. “No powder mix?”


“Baby girl, you’re in the South. Powdered lemonade is a crime.”


They stepped into the house, and it hit her—wooden floors, braided rugs, the smell of fresh bread and roasted garlic, sunflowers in a vase, and actual framed family photos.


It was… cozy.


Warm.


And wildly unfamiliar.


Grace led her upstairs to a small but charming room—white lace curtains, a soft bed, a wooden dresser with a mirror that made her look like a Disney princess.


“I hope it’s okay,” Grace said, watching her.


Alara walked over to the bed, gently touched the quilt, and nodded. “It’s beautiful. Thank you.”


She didn’t mean to sound so soft. But something in the way Grace treated her—like she mattered, not because of how she looked or what she wore, but just because she was—got under her skin.


“Dinner’s at six,” Grace said. “You’ll be eatin’ with us, so don’t go running off. And don’t let my son scare you. He growls a lot, but he’s a good boy.”


Alara tilted her head. “Does he ever smile?”


“Oh, honey,” Grace chuckled, “you’d have better luck getting a pig to fly.”


Alara laughed, then sat down on the edge of the bed. She pulled off her sunglasses and sighed.


So far, it wasn’t as bad as she’d feared.


The mom was a sweetheart.


The house was adorable.


And River, while grumpy and annoyingly hot, was tolerable. Kind of...... Maybe?


Later that Evening


Dinner was roasted chicken, corn on the cob, mashed potatoes, and hot biscuits smothered in honey butter.


Alara didn’t just eat—she devoured, abandoning all table manners halfway through the biscuits.


“Don’t tell anyone”, she whispered to Grace, licking honey off her fingers, “but this is better than Nobu.”


River stared at her like she’d just said the moon was made of cheese.


“What the hell is Nobu?”


She froze, fork in hand. “You uncultured swine.”


Grace cackled and nearly choked on her tea.


River rolled his eyes. “Tomorrow, you’re helping me with the coop.”


Alara’s face twisted. “Coop? What coop?”


“The chicken coop.”


Her jaw dropped. “You want me to go near demon birds with claws and flappy wings?”


River leaned back in his chair, smug. “Yup.”


Alara looked at Grace in betrayal. “I thought you loved me.”


Grace just smiled. “Welcome to the countryside, sugar.”
